# Annie's Magic Numbers

Profit & margin analysis on Annie's 2016 wholesale liquor data.  Answers
the three questions she asked:

  1. Top 10 products by profit ($) and by margin (%).
  2. Top 10 brands by profit ($) and by margin (%).
  3. Which brands / products to drop because they lose money.

The deliverable is a single Markdown report (`reports/REPORT.md`) plus
the underlying CSVs in `reports/`.  Headline (from a clean run on the
PwC case-study CSVs):

| Revenue | COGS | Excise tax | Net profit | Net margin |
|--------:|-----:|-----------:|-----------:|-----------:|
| $452.1M | $313.4M | $19.0M | $119.7M | 26.5% |

## Run it

1. Drop the six PwC case-study CSVs into `src/`.
2. Confirm `bin/duckdb.exe` is present (it is — vendored at v1.1.3).
3. Run:

   ```
   python analysis/run.py
   ```

End-to-end runtime ~17 s on a laptop, dominated by parsing the 1.7 GB
sales CSV.  Re-runs are equally fast — the bottleneck is CSV parse, not
analysis, so caching the DB buys little until the data grows another
order of magnitude.

No third-party Python deps.  Tested against Python 3.10+ (the
orchestrator uses only stdlib + the DuckDB CLI subprocess).

## Decisions and why

### Why DuckDB, not pandas / Postgres / SQLite

The sales file alone is ~1.7 GB (12.8 M rows).  The shape of the
analysis is "scan a big table, group, join a small dimension" — a
classic columnar-aggregation workload.

  * **Pandas** would load row-wise into RAM, re-allocate during dtype
    inference, and burn memory we do not need.
  * **SQLite** is fine for the size but row-oriented; group-bys against
    12 M rows are noticeably slower.
  * **Postgres** is overkill: needs a server, a schema, a `COPY`
    command, and a vacuum afterwards for a one-shot analysis.
  * **DuckDB** reads CSVs directly (vectorized, parallel), runs SQL,
    and persists to a single file.  No server, no schema migration,
    one binary.

The local Python (3.15 alpha) had no precompiled DuckDB wheel and
building from source failed (no compiler).  Rather than downgrading
Python, I vendored the 10 MB DuckDB CLI binary and wrote a Python
orchestrator that shells out to it.  The SQL is the analysis; Python
is glue.

### Profit / margin formula

For each sales line:

```
revenue       = SalesDollars
unit_cost     = weighted-avg cost paid in 2016 for this (Brand, Size)
                = sum(Purchase.Dollars) / sum(Purchase.Quantity)
                  (fall back to catalog PurchasePrice if SKU not
                   purchased in-period)
cogs          = SalesQuantity * unit_cost
excise_tax    = ExciseTax (paid by the wholesaler to the state)

gross_profit  = revenue - cogs
net_profit    = revenue - cogs - excise_tax
gross_margin  = gross_profit / revenue
net_margin    = net_profit   / revenue
```

I aggregate at two grains:

  * **Product** = Brand × Size — that is the actual stocking unit.
  * **Brand**   = the `Brand` column rolled across sizes — that is what
    Annie probably means colloquially.  (The dataset's `Brand` is not a
    "brand family" name; it is a SKU id, e.g. 58 → "Gekkeikan Black &
    Gold Sake".  But within a Brand id the product is the same liquid
    in different bottles, which is the right rollup for "drop this
    brand?" decisions.)

### Why weighted-average cost rather than inventory roll-forward

The accounting view says
`COGS = BegInv + Purchases - EndInv`.  That gives a top-line COGS but
no per-SKU split.  Per-SKU you need a unit-cost model (FIFO, LIFO, or
weighted average).  I used weighted average because:

  * It is unbiased over a 12-month window where Annie's costs do not
    swing dramatically.
  * It is computable from the data we have (no inventory layers).
  * It reconciles cleanly: the two methods agree to within 0.0 % at the
    company level (see "Sanity checks" in REPORT.md).

### Which margin to rank on (gross vs. net)

I use **net margin** for rankings and the drop list.  Excise tax is
roughly $19 M of $452 M revenue — about 4 % — and it is a real cash
outflow Annie makes on every sale.  Excluding it would flatter low-
margin spirits where excise is a meaningful share of cost.  Both gross
and net are surfaced in every CSV in case the accountant wants gross.

### Filters on the rankings

  * **Profit ranking** — no filter; the whole point is volume + margin
    combined.
  * **Margin ranking** — minimum revenue of $10 K (products) / $25 K
    (brands), AND `cogs > 0`, AND no missing cost matches.  Without
    these guards the top-margin list fills with one-off mis-coded
    sales: my first run had Macallan at #1 with a "98.6 % margin"
    because we found no cost row for it and divided by zero.  Filtering
    those out kept the list believable.
  * **Drop list** — net loss, >= 12 units sold (~once a month, rules
    out one-off mis-prices), >= $500 revenue per product / $1 K per
    brand.  Items with unreconciled cost are also excluded — a "loss"
    against a missing cost is meaningless.

### Things I deliberately did NOT do

  * **No per-store breakdown.** Annie asked about products and brands.
    The data has store, city, vendor — happy to extend, but adding it
    to the headline answer would be scope creep.
  * **No statistical significance / time-series detrending.** A full
    year of data is enough to make these calls; weekly granularity
    would matter if Annie wanted a "trend / seasonality" view.
  * **No fancy visualizations.** Markdown tables travel everywhere
    (email, Slack, PR review).  The CSVs are there if anyone wants to
    pivot in Excel / Tableau.
  * **No write-up of operational recommendations.** Annie asked which
    SKUs lose money, not what to do about each one.  Some "drops"
    (Southern Comfort 50mL minis, Bacardi 375mL) are loss leaders that
    drive trips and may be worth keeping; that is a buyer's decision,
    not the data's.  The report flags the candidates; she chooses.

## What surprised me in the data

  * **30.7 % gross margin on $452 M of wholesale liquor is healthy.**
    Wholesale spirits typically run 15–25 % gross.  Either Annie's mix
    is unusually high-end or the upstream "Iowa-style" data set folds
    in a markup that is partly retail.  Worth a clarifying conversation
    with her before treating the absolute number as gospel.
  * **The 50 mL "mini" SKUs are margin monsters.** Top-margin product
    is Jack Daniels Tennessee Fire 50mL at 73 %.  Several others at
    55 %+.  Likely impulse-buy pricing power.
  * **The drop list skews wine-heavy.** Most of the worst losses are
    obscure varietals (High Valley Zinfandel, Feudi Di San Gregorio
    Fiano, Buehler Zin Napa) — slow movers priced at or below cost.
  * **Cost coverage is essentially complete.** Only 295 of 12.8 M
    sales rows ($19 K of $452 M revenue) lack a cost match.  Good
    data quality — better than I assumed going in.

## Limitations and follow-ups

  * **Excise-tax allocation is per sales line.** If Annie's contract
    actually fixes excise as a per-bottle dollar amount that does not
    scale with price, results would shift slightly.  The dataset's
    `ExciseTax` column is what I trusted.
  * **`Brand` is a SKU id, not a brand family.** True "rolled-up"
    brand-family analysis (e.g. all Jack Daniels variants together)
    would need a fuzzy match on `Description`.  Worth doing as a
    follow-up if Annie wants brand-portfolio decisions.
  * **The 30.7 % gross margin needs a sanity check with Annie.**
    Whatever the answer, it tells us something about the data
    (markup baked in?) or the business (premium mix?).
  * **No cost trend / mid-year price changes.** A weighted-year
    average smooths over a price spike.  If Annie expects vendor cost
    inflation in 2017, an end-of-year cost would be more conservative.
